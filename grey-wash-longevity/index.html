The method

Grey wash that heals

The hardest part of black & grey isn't making it look good on day one. It's making it still read in ten years. This is the method the Academy is built around.

Why black & grey portraits fade into grey

A portrait that looks razor-sharp the day it's tattooed can turn into a soft grey smudge within a couple of years: the black lightens, the contrast collapses, and the features stop reading. The usual causes are contrast built too subtly to survive the skin's healing, and grey tones laid without enough separation between values.

True grey wash — black and water

Federico builds his greys from black ink diluted with water — not pre-mixed grey ink. Controlled dilution gives a wider, cleaner range of values and more predictable healing. The method is about building contrast that survives: holding the darkest darks opaque, protecting the highlights, and spacing the mid-tones so the face still separates after the tattoo settles.

How to judge a black & grey portrait

  • Not fresh — healed. Fresh work always looks contrasty; the real test is how it reads once it has settled.
  • Value separation: can you still tell the planes of the face apart from across the room?
  • Opaque darks and protected highlights — the two anchors that keep a portrait legible as it ages.

This longevity-first approach is the throughline of all three days of the masterclass.

Questions

Does diluting ink with water make tattoos fade faster?

Not when it's done with control — the point of the method is greys that heal predictably and hold their separation over time.

Can you learn to make black & grey heal better?

Yes — it's a system of value control and contrast, not innate talent. That system is exactly what this masterclass teaches.
